Predicting Housing Sales

Team Jai Ho Midterm

Published

October 26, 2025

Project Overview: Our team was tasked by the Philadelphia Office of Property Assessment with the mission to improve upon the Automated Valuation Model (AVM) to deliver more accurate property tax assessments. In the end, we built a model that accurately predicts house sale prices in the Philadelphia are using structural, Census, and spatial variables. The goal is to use our model to support data-driven policy decisions related in the city to deliver equitable property assessments and increase fairness, trust, etc. fo all residents.

Members: Jack Bader, Joey Cahill, Matthew Levy, Sam Sen, Tim Wen, Ye Zhang

Date: October 27, 2025

Project Intention

Why it Matters

Philadelphia’s current Automated Valuation Model (AVM) is underperforming, leading to inconsistent and inequitable property assessments.

Our Guiding Question

What key predictors are missing from the legacy model, and how can we improve its accuracy and fairness?

Structural Price Drivers

Progressive Regression Models

Goal: Test whether place-based factors improve predictive accuracy.

\[ \begin{aligned} \log(\text{Price}) = \beta_0 &+ \color{red}{\beta_1 \log(\text{Livable Area}) + \beta_2 \text{Bedrooms} + \beta_3 \text{Bathrooms}} \\ &+ \color{red}{\beta_4 \text{Age} + \beta_5 \text{Interior Condition} + \beta_6 \text{Quality Grade}} \\ &+ \color{orange}{\beta_7 \text{Median Income} + \beta_8 \text{Family Household Ratio}} \\ &+ \color{orange}{\beta_9 \text{% Bachelors}+ \beta_{10} \text{% Vacant} + \beta_{11} \text{% White}} \\ &+ \color{blue}{\beta_{12} \text{EconKDE} + \beta_{13} \text{EconKDE}^2 + \beta_{14} \text{TreeKDE} + \beta_{15} \text{Mean 3NN Dist}} \\ &+ \color{green}{\beta_{16} \text{MHI Quantile} + \beta_{17} (\text{MHI Quantile} \times \text{Age})} + \varepsilon \end{aligned} \]

Model Layers
- Model 1: Structural
- Model 2: + Demographics
- Model 3: + Spatial
- Model 4: + Interactions

Model Performance Improves at Each Stage

Model Adj. R² Interpretation
(1) Structural 0.38 House features alone are not enough
(2) + Census 0.60 Neighborhood socioeconomic patterns matter
(3) + Spatial 0.61 Location and amenities add signal
(4) + Interactions 0.64 Wealthy areas shape how homes retain value

Takeaway: Spatial + neighborhood effects are essential to explaining price variation in Philadelphia.

Hardest Neighborhoods to Predict

Key Insight

  • The model struggles most in high-value, high-amenity neighborhoods
  • Under-predicted (red): Center City, Northwest Philly, Fishtown, South Philly
    → Homes sell for more than the model expects
  • Over-predicted (blue): Economically underserved neighborhoods
    → Homes sell for less than predicted
  • Why? Prestige, walkability, and growing interest are not fully captured by structural or census variables

Key Findings

Model Accuracy: RMSE = 0.479 (log scale)
Model Fit: R² = 0.64 (0.65 with K-fold CV)

Top Predictors

  • Economic Density (U-shaped effect): Strongest spatial driver of price
  • Home Size (β = 0.509, p < 0.001): 1% increase in area → ~0.5% ↑ in price
  • Bathrooms (β = 0.151, p < 0.001): Large value premium per added bath
  • Neighborhood Composition: Higher education (+) and vacancy (–) strongly shape prices

Key Insight:
Neighborhood effects matter as much as home features — location premiums are real, measurable, and spatially clustered. —

Recommendations

Improve the City’s AVM by Incorporating:

  • Amenity-based spatial features (EconKDE, trees, school access)
  • Neighborhood context variables to capture local market conditions

Policy Implications

  • Current AVM likely undervalues booming neighborhoods near economic hubs
  • Affluent areas remain hardest to predict due to prestige and perception effects
  • Add monitoring for rapidly changing neighborhoods (e.g., Fishtown)

Bottom Line:
To reduce inequity and improve accuracy, the AVM must move beyond structure-only predictors and account for context, amenities, and spatial clustering.

Limitations & Next Steps

Key Limitations

  • The model cannot quantify word-of-mouth effects
    → Perceived “vibe shifts” can rapidly change prices
  • Future plans are not incorporated
    → Planned infrastructure can greatly alter market outcomes

Where Do We Go From Here?

  • Build an enhanced model that includes:
    → Amenity proximity
    → Economic development indicators
    → Spatial clustering of sale prices

  • Continue data collection to track a changing housing landscape

  • Consult with local government on new projects and emerging trends
